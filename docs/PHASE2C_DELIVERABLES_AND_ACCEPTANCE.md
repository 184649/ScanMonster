## 2. Stage 0 — Creative direction

Before drawing, submit:

- a one-sentence statement of the WORLDAWN character IP direction;
- shape principles shared by all three characters;
- shape principles that must vary among them;
- treatment of eyes and faces without one-face reuse;
- direction for line, shading, and color planes;
- a method for differentiating the work from existing IP;
- a one-sentence core and reason to like each character;
- the proposed silhouette, face, and color memory points;
- reasons for any proposed motif replacement.

**Acceptance:** the direction explains a discovery-centered, non-combat IP; gives the three different body systems a common visual logic; identifies specific prohibited shortcuts; and supports future expansion. The user must approve Stage 0 in writing before finished illustration or Stage 1 execution proceeds. A motif replacement also requires separate user approval.

## 3. Stage 1 — Black silhouettes

Submit **8–12 independent concepts per character**. Each must be a separate character proposal, not a pose variation.

Requirements:

- one solid black shape on white, with no internal detail;
- no eyes, expression, color, texture, shading, equipment, lettering, or background;
- one primary memory point per concept;
- meaningful variation in head/body ratio, center of mass, outer contour, posture, species-specific parts, and major-block organization;
- full body visible and legible at 64 px;
- feasible conversion into a toy.

Deliver:

- a comparison board;
- an independent image for every option;
- a one-sentence character core per option;
- a one-sentence reason to like each option;
- a short explanation of how each option is structurally different.

**Acceptance and user decision:** technical review confirms compliance, but does not rank charm or commercial appeal. The user selects up to two options per motif. If none can be selected, the parties use only the agreed revision/reproposal scope or stop the commission. No automatic fallback and no Stage 2 without a selection.

## 4. Stage 2 — Head, face, and expression

Normally begin after narrowing each motif to one silhouette. Deliver:

- front, three-quarter, and side head views;
- neutral, joy, alert, puzzled, and calm expressions;
- eyes closed and mouth open states;
- construction notes showing how face planes join the species-specific head and body.

**Acceptance:** the face cannot be exchanged with another animal; it is not overly human; personality reads without a paragraph of explanation; required features remain legible on a small screen; the three characters do not share one face; and no view strongly resembles a specific famous character. User and designer approve the construction before color work.

## 5. Stage 3 — Color-plane design

Submit about three color-plane directions per character.

Requirements:

- primarily three or four large colors/regions;
- no reliance on fine patterns;
- readable in grayscale and identifiable at 128 px;
- not strongly associated with the signature palette/marking arrangement of a specific existing IP;
- normal-rarity designs remain appealing without drifting into rare/legendary ornament;
- reproducible in physical merchandise.

**Acceptance and user decision:** provide 128 px and grayscale proofs plus color specifications. The user selects the final direction. No selection means no Stage 4.

## 6. Stage 4 — Model sheet

Deliver:

- front, side, rear, and three-quarter views;
- top or other necessary auxiliary view;
- expression sheet;
- breakdown of major shapes;
- color specification;
- lineup/size comparison;
- 64 px and 128 px proofs;
- simplified construction for toy production;
- prohibited deformations;
- left/right asymmetry;
- movement, articulation, and pose cautions.

**Acceptance:** all views preserve the approved silhouette, face, proportions, asymmetry, and memory points; the construction can be reproduced by another artist; small-size tests pass; and the form can be organized into approximately 8–12 major three-dimensional blocks. Model-sheet approval is required before key art.

## 7. Stage 5 — Finished key art

Begin only after model-sheet approval. Deliver:

- at least 1024 × 1024 pixels;
- transparent background with a genuine alpha channel;
- no cropped major parts;
- no background, ground, or foot shadow;
- a composition that can generate a thumbnail;
- editable source data with linked/embedded assets accounted for;
- a flattened integrated PNG;
- vector or higher-resolution source when the agreed production use requires it.

**Acceptance:** art approval and technical file validation are separate. Confirm the approved construction, image dimensions, alpha, margin, complete PNG structure, source-file editability, color profile, font/material provenance, and agreed naming/version convention. A technically valid PNG is not automatically approved art.

## 8. Stage 6 — Cross-character art bible

Deliver a three-character system covering:

- common silhouette rules and character-specific variation rules;
- head/body ratio, face, eyes, mouth, ears, limbs, line, shading, color planes, texture, pose, and expression;
- how `normal`, `rare`, and `legendary` differ without becoming quality tiers;
- prohibited constructions and transformations;
- a new-character design template;
- full-size, 128 px, and 64 px lineup proofs;
- reference material that could govern future human work and any separately approved AI-assisted work.

**Acceptance:** the trio reads as one world while retaining distinct faces, eyes, body architectures, silhouettes, and reasons to like them. The system must work beyond mammals and be actionable for another artist. Bulk work toward 24 characters remains blocked until Stage 6 passes.

## 9. Character acceptance checklist

Each character is reviewed against all of the following:

1. **Three-second memory:** after three seconds, a viewer can describe one feature unique to the character.
2. **Silhouette:** the three are distinguishable using black outer shapes only.
3. **Personality:** posture and construction suggest personality without the written brief.
4. **Reason to like:** the choice can be explained beyond “cute” or “cool.”
5. **Face exchange:** moving the face to another animal breaks the design.
6. **Small screen:** face and primary memory point read at 64 px and 128 px.
7. **Toy translation:** the character can be organized into approximately 8–12 major three-dimensional blocks.
8. **Drawability:** a child or fan can preserve its primary feature in a simplified drawing.
9. **IP coherence:** the trio belongs together without sharing one face, eye, or body type.
10. **Existing-IP similarity:** no design strongly recalls a specific famous character; specialist rights review may still be needed.
11. **User judgment:** beside popular characters, the user still wants to see it completed, feels they could like it, and might choose it.

The final user judgment is not scored or replaced by AI, automation, the numerical portfolio rubric, or technical QA.
